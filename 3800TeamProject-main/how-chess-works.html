Step 1: How To Setup The Chessboard


Every player starts with the white (or light) color square in the bottom right-hand square of the chessboard.

The chess pieces are then always placed in the same configuration. There are pawns in the second row (or rank). The rooks go in the corners, followed by the knights beside them, the bishops, and lastly the queen, who always goes on her corresponding color (black queen on black, white queen on white), with the king going on the last square.

Step 2: How The Chess Pieces Move


Every one of the six types of pieces moves in a unique way. Pieces are never allowed to move onto a square that contains one of their own pieces, nor can they pass through other pieces (although the knight can leap over other pieces). They can, however, be moved to replace a piece that an opponent has captured after it has been moved. Generally, pieces are placed so they can control key squares in the game, defend themselves against capture, or capture other pieces by landing on their square and then replacing them.

How to move the Pawn

Pawns are unique in that they move forward but capture diagonally, which is a different way of moving and capturing. Pawns can only advance one square at a time, with the exception of their initial move, which allows them to advance two squares. Only one diagonal square can be captured by pawns in front of them. They are unable to move or move backward. A pawn is unable to move past or capture a piece that is placed directly in front of him.

How to move the Knight

Knights move very differently from other pieces; they move in a straight line for two squares and then make one more move at a 90-degree angle, forming an L. Knights are also the only pieces that can move over other pieces.

How to move the Bishop

p> The bishop can move in any direction, but only in a diagonal path. Every bishop must always remain on the same color—either light or dark—from the beginning. Bishops complement one another well by covering one another's weaknesses.

How to move the Rook

The only directions the rook can move are forward, backward, and to the sides. When the rooks are cooperating and defending one another, they are especially strong pieces!

How to move the Queen

The most powerful piece is the queen. She can move as far as she can in any straight direction, as long as she avoids going through any of her own pieces. These directions include forward, backward, sideways, and diagonal. The queen's move ends if she captures an opponent's piece, just like it does with other pieces.

How to move the King

Despite being one of the weakest pieces, the king is the most crucial. The king can only go one square in all directions, including diagonally, up, down, and sideways. The king might never put himself in a position where he could be apprehended. This is known as a "check" when the king is under attack from another piece.

Step 3: Discover The Special Rules Of Chess

Chess has a few unique rules that might not make sense at first. They were developed in order to add interest and fun to the game. These rules are:


  • Castling

    • One unique chess rule is known as castling. With this move, you can potentially accomplish two key objectives in one move: moving your rook out of the corner and into play, and hopefully getting your king to safety. A player may move his king two squares to one side during his turn, and he may then move the rook from the corner of that side to the square directly next to the king on the other side. (See the video below.) But in order to castle, the following requirements have to be fulfilled:


    • it must be that king's very first move
    • it must be that rook's very first move
    • there cannot be any pieces between the king and rook to move
    • the king may not be in check or pass through check

    You'll notice that the king is closer to the side of the board when you castle in one direction. We refer to that as "kingside" casting. Castling "queenside" refers to casting to the opposite side, through which the queen sat. When castling, the king always moves only two squares, regardless of which side is involved.



  • Promotion

    • A pawn's ability to become any other chess piece, with the exception of a king, is a unique ability known as promotion. If a pawn reaches the other side of the board, then this ability activates and allows the pawn to change.


    A pawn is able to become a queen, rook, bishop, or knight. It's a frequent misperception that pawns can only be traded for captured pieces. That is untrue. Typically, a pawn advances to a queen. You can only promote pawns.


  • "En Passant"

    • Another pawn rule is known as "en passant," which translates to "in passing" in French. The other pawn can choose to capture the first pawn as it moves past if the pawn moves out two squares on its first move and lands to the side of the opponent's pawn, effectively jumping past its ability to be captured. Once the first pawn has moved past, you have to make this special move right away or else you will lose the opportunity to capture it.


Step 4: Find Out Who Makes The First Move In Chess


Every time, the player using the white pieces moves first. Therefore, in most games, the decision of who gets to be white is made by chance or happenstance, such as when a coin is flipped or a player is asked to guess the color of a hidden pawn in the other player's hand. After that, until the game is over, white moves first, then black, then white again, and so on. The small advantage of being able to move first allows the white player to launch an immediate attack.

Step 6: Study Basic Chess Strategies

There are four simple strategies that every chess player should know:

Protect Your King

Get your king to the corner of the board where he is usually safer. Don't put off castling. You should usually castle as quickly as possible. Remember, it doesn't matter how close you are to checkmating your opponent if your own king is checkmated first!

Don't Give Pieces Away

Try not to lose your pieces! You need all of the pieces to checkmate in order to win a game, and each piece has value. Most players use a simple system to remember how much each chess piece is worth in relation to the others. What is the value of the chess pieces?


  • A pawn is worth 1 point
  • A knight is worth 3 points
  • A bishop is worth 3 points
  • A rook is worth 5 points
  • A queen is worth 9 points
  • The king is infinitely valuable

These points have no impact on the outcome of the game; they are just a tool for decision-making during gameplay, assisting you in determining when to capture, trade, or execute other actions.

Control The Center Of The Chessboard

With your pieces and pawns, you should attempt to take control of the center of the board. Controlling the center will give you more space to maneuver your pieces and make it more difficult for your opponent to locate advantageous squares for his pieces. In the aforementioned example, black plays poor moves while white makes smart moves to control the center.

Use All Of Your Chess Pieces

When your pieces are relegated to the first row, they are of no use. In order to have more pieces available for the king attack, try to develop every piece you have. Attacking with one or two pieces will not work against any competitive opponent.
